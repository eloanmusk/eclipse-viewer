What is an Eclipse?

An eclipse is the partial or total obscuration of one celestial body by another. In our solar system, the most spectacular eclipses arise from a remarkable cosmic coincidence — the Sun is almost precisely 400 times the diameter of the Moon, while also being approximately 400 times more distant. This causes both to subtend nearly equal angles in our sky, making total solar eclipses possible.

Astronomers use the term syzygy (Greek: "yoked together") for the near-perfect alignment of three celestial bodies. When this occurs at a lunar node — the intersection of the Moon's orbital plane with the ecliptic — we witness an eclipse.

Solar Eclipse

The Moon passes between Earth and Sun, casting its umbral shadow on Earth. Totality can last up to 7 minutes 31 seconds at a single point.

Lunar Eclipse

Earth interposes itself between Sun and Moon, projecting its much larger shadow onto the lunar surface. Visible wherever the Moon is above the horizon.

A Taxonomy of Eclipses

Solar Eclipses — classified by the geometry of the Moon's shadow falling upon Earth:

🌑

Total

Moon fully covers the Sun. The solar corona becomes visible. Rare at any given location.

💍

Annular

Moon near apogee appears too small. A luminous "Ring of Fire" surrounds the dark disk.

🌗

Partial

Only the penumbral shadow falls on the observer. A partial bite is taken from the Sun.

Lunar Eclipses — classified by depth of the Moon's passage into Earth's shadow:

🔴

Total

Moon fully in Earth's umbra. Refracted sunlight turns it deep crimson — the "Blood Moon."

🌓

Partial

Part of the Moon enters the umbra. A dark curved bite is plainly visible to the naked eye.

🌕

Penumbral

Moon traverses only the penumbra. The darkening is subtle and often overlooked.

Celestial Mechanics

The Moon's orbital plane is inclined approximately 5.145° to the ecliptic. This tilt ensures most New and Full Moons pass above or below the alignment required for an eclipse. Eclipses occur only when the Moon is near a lunar node, which precess westward over an 18.6-year cycle.

The Moon's elliptical orbit produces two critical distances: perigee (~356,500 km) when its disk is large enough to fully cover the Sun, producing totals; and apogee (~406,700 km) when it appears smaller and annular eclipses result.

The Great Coincidence

Sun diameter: 1,392,700 km at 149.6M km. Moon diameter: 3,474 km at 384,400 km. Both subtend ~0.5° — a coincidence unique in the known solar system.

Eclipse Seasons

Eclipses come in "seasons" every ~173 days when the Sun approaches a lunar node. Each season lasts ~38 days and may contain 1–3 eclipse events.

The Saros Cycle

Discovered by ancient Babylonians, the Saros is a period of 6,585.3211 days (18 years, 11 days, 8 hours) after which the Sun, Earth, and Moon return to nearly identical relative positions, causing eclipses to repeat in predictable series.

The Saros arises from the commensurability of three lunar periods: 223 synodic months, 239 anomalistic months, and 242 draconic months — all nearly equal to 6,585.32 days. The extra 8 hours shifts each eclipse 120° westward in longitude. After three Saros cycles (the Exeligmos — 54 years, 34 days), eclipses reappear over nearly the same geography.

Active Series

~40 Saros series run simultaneously. Each spans 1,200–1,500 years, containing 70–80 eclipses. The Sun gains or loses about 0.5° per Saros in declination.

Historical Power

Hipparchus (~130 BC) used the Saros to predict lunar eclipses centuries before Newtonian mechanics. Babylonian tablets recording Saros series date to 747 BC.

The Historical Record

585 BC — The eclipse of Thales. The philosopher predicted it using Babylonian records, halting the Battle of Halys mid-combat. One of the oldest precisely dated events in all of history.

1504 — Columbus, stranded in Jamaica, foreknew a lunar eclipse from his almanac. He used the "Blood Moon" to frighten the Arawak into resuming his crew's food supply.

1868 — Jules Janssen detected an unknown spectral line in the corona during totality. The element — helium — was not found on Earth until 1895.

1919 — Eddington photographed stars near the eclipsed Sun from Príncipe Island, confirming Einstein's General Theory of Relativity by measuring the bending of starlight around the Sun.

2024 — The Great North American Eclipse of April 8th crossed 15 US states. An estimated 32 million people experienced totality — the most-watched eclipse in recorded history.
